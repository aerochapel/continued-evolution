Existential Metric

20 May 2025

Most people don’t realize how much of life is performative until it’s too late. They follow the script. They listen to authority. They respond with polite nods instead of instinct. And they survive, just long enough to forget what it means to live.

I learned this during the most physically dangerous moment of my life, when my appendix burst, and no one believed me.

1. Instinct First

I was on my way to Vietnam with my (now ex-) wife. She went ahead. I deliberately didn’t get the visa. Something in me knew I shouldn’t go. I didn’t have the language to explain it, just a quiet resistance. That resistance saved my life.

Had I gone to Hanoi, I would have been isolated, linguistically blocked, and without support when my appendix burst. I would have died, not from infection, but from the system’s delay. Instead, I was denied entry, sent back to South Korea, and within 12 hours of arriving back, I was in excruciating pain.

It was my 30th birthday.

2. The Knife

When I was being rerouted from Vietnam, I had a Swiss Army knife in my checked luggage, something I’d owned since I was eight years old. It was given to me in the south of France, though it came from Switzerland. I used it to carve out my early projects in my dad’s woodshed. Real things. Made with my hands. With attention. With care.

At the airport, they told me I had to dispose of it. No exception. No negotiation. It was discarded like a convenience item, something forgettable.

That moment didn’t just strip me of a tool. It stripped away a symbol of everything I’d spent my life preserving — precision, memory, individuality, the ability to shape the world instead of passively accept it. And I watched it drop into a bin.

3. The Misalignment Begins

Back in Korea, I told the doctors something was wrong. I knew it wasn’t indigestion. I could feel the distinction. I could feel the escalation. But they didn’t believe me. They looked at me like I was dramatic. Or uninformed. Or both.

It didn’t matter how accurate my instinct was. It didn’t matter how precisely I described the symptoms. Because in their world, the patient should never know more than the physician. To be ahead is to be threatening.

They expected me to come like a pale of water — empty, passive, ready to be filled. But I had already done the work. I already knew what this was. And that intimidated them more than the problem itself.

4. I Sat With It Alone

So I sat with a burst appendix. Alone. In silence.

The physical pain matched the deeper truth: this is what it’s like to live in a world where your insight is disbelieved. To carry knowledge while being told you’re imagining it. To warn others while they smile politely and change the subject. To build something in response to what’s coming, and be dismissed until it already arrives.

When they finally realized the truth — that my appendix had already ruptured, that bacteria had spread — they rushed me into surgery. No apology. No reflection. Just a shift from disbelief to urgency. The pattern repeated itself quietly, unexamined.

I spent Christmas and New Year alone in the hospital.

5. The Same Pattern in My Work

That same pattern plays out every time I release a project.

I build something with depth. And that depth comes from decades of refinement — from staying in my room while the other kids followed trends, from being told by my parents that I was strange, from being asked why I spent so much time alone. What I was doing was simple. I was pursuing truth.

The education system tells us that we go to school to improve — to accelerate our intellectual evolution. But if that evolution is only allowed to happen inside that space, what happens when you continue it on your own?

I was doing it extracurricularly. I was evolving in my own time. And that’s what made me threatening.

Because I wasn’t just learning. I was refining. And I was watching a world I knew would collapse under its own weight — and now, that collapse is visibly unfolding, especially in places like the UK. A country run by people with no foresight, supported by citizens with no instinct for fundamentals.

And I have to quietly sit in the margins, pretending the things I’ve seen and said aren’t real, because they can’t be branded into something that will make people say anything other than “you think too much.”

6. The Real Work Is Integrity

My instinct saved my life once. Now, every day, it saves my integrity.

If I don’t protect that, I’m not living — I’m just performing. And my life has never been performative.

What I build now — Cognography, BODIN, Honshitsu — isn’t performance. It’s not branding. It’s correction.

I’m not trying to express myself through theatrics. I express myself through systems. Through structural integrity. And when that’s dismissed or ignored, I have to ask myself: What kind of reality am I living in?

Because I’ve always been honest in what I put out. And when people say they want “original thinkers” or “passionate creators,” and then they can’t recognize it when it stands in front of them — you realize it was all performance.

They wouldn’t recognize originality even if it interrupted their entire workflow.

7. This Is the Metric

So what is the Existential Metric?

It’s not a feeling. It’s not a belief. It’s the distance between what you know and what the world is willing to accept. It’s the pain of being right too soon. It’s the ache of refinement in a culture of simulation. It’s the silence that follows clarity.

And it’s the responsibility to keep building anyway.

8. The Last Word

This is what it’s like to build things that are misunderstood. And even when you write about them — articulate them clearly — they’re still misunderstood. Because some of us can’t express ourselves through performance or personality. We build our language in the architecture of systems.

And when that language is ignored, dismissed, or misread, we carry that knowledge silently.

We carry it like a burst appendix — something no one believes is real until the damage is already done.
