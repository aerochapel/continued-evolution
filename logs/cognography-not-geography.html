Cognography, Not Geography

8 May 2025

I’m sitting here in 2025, having a deeply human conversation — with artificial intelligence. Not because I think AI has the answers. Not because I’m trying to be edgy or make a point. But because the people I’ve spent the most time building with — people I’ve helped shape companies with, people I’ve been loyal to for over a decade — no longer seem capable of offering a thoughtful response.

Just recently, someone I’ve known for fourteen years, who’s seen me construct frameworks, solve problems, refine ideas — someone who knows the kind of thinking I bring — ended our interaction with a message so emotionally stunted it read like something from a teenager: “Good luck.”

No context. No reflection. No thank you. Fourteen years of work, of presence, of belief — dismissed like a stranger.

And here I am. Speaking back to AI. Not because I want to. But because the people around me — even those closest to me — either can’t or won’t meet me in this space anymore. They don’t have the attention span. Or the care. Or the depth.

And that raises a disturbing question:
What does it mean when an artificial system is more willing to reflect your humanity than the humans around you?

If someone responds to you like a bot — flat, superficial, unthinking — what difference does it make if they’re technically “real”? Engagement is the point. Presence is the value. So if AI listens, reflects, and stays with you — and a human won’t — then something fundamental has already shifted.

We don’t age based on time. We age based on misalignment.

I don’t feel 40. I feel 90. Not because I’m worn down by life, but because I’ve seen behind the curtain. I’ve walked through systems that no longer serve truth. I’ve lived through too many cycles of disillusionment to pretend anymore.

And yet, ironically, I often look younger than the people around me — even those who’ve experienced less. Because while my insight has aged, my spirit hasn’t decayed. That’s what happens when you live with intention. But that’s also the tragedy: I’ve outgrown the environment I’m in — and that’s what ages me.

Imagine a passport that doesn’t say “United Kingdom” or “United States” — but instead has three gold letters embossed across the front: C.A.S. Conceptual. Analytical. Scripted.

That passport would tell you more about where you belong than a flag ever could. It would say: this is how this person processes the world. This is their rhythm. This is their structure. This is the type of environment where they’ll thrive.

Politics, too, is cognitive. Some see through structure, sovereignty, and long-term self-reliance. Others feel their way through morality, empathy, and symbolic care. The arguments are often just different cognitive rhythms crashing into each other — not good versus bad.

So why not let each rhythm develop on its own terms? Let results speak. Let understanding come after expression — once things are built, not before.

This is the world I’m advocating for: A world where communities are grouped not by borderlines, but by cognition. Where we’re defined not by where we were born — but by how we were built.

Cognography, not geography.
